I've been reflecting more and more on the different types of companies out there.
The more places and people I see, the more I keep sorting organizations and jobs
into buckets — and I think it basically comes down to two.

My first version of this was "community orgs versus non-community companies," and I
think that holds true for a bit. But the more I sat with it, the more I think the
real split is something else.

The first bucket is the high-output companies. Think Meta, think Google. High
output, high pay, high stress, a lot of crazy meaningful stuff happening, just a
*lot* going on. At those places, it's really about the work. And interestingly, I
think the same thing holds at really small companies — at a tiny company, you
literally can't afford to not be having results. Different size, same deal: the
work is the point, and everything else arranges itself around that.

Then there's the other bucket, and I don't have a fancy name for it: it's people
living their life. The job is good enough for most people. People can pay their
bills. Nobody's pretending it's a rocket ship, and it doesn't need to be. And
here's the part that's been sitting with me — when that kind of company works, it
becomes a community.

That's the piece I keep coming back to. I think we all need work so much more than
we realize — not just for the paycheck, but for the sense of being part of the
world. Being part of something. Being *relevant*. You show up somewhere, people
know you, what you do matters to somebody — that's carrying way more weight in
people's lives than we usually give it credit for. And the living-your-life bucket
of companies might be quietly providing that better than anyone talks about.

I want to be clear that I don't think there's a right answer here. This isn't a
"which bucket should you pick" thing. It's just a thing to reflect on — that so
much of life is just being part of a community and being relevant, and work is one
of the main places we get that, whichever bucket you're standing in.

Still working this one out. But I keep noticing it, so it felt worth writing down.
